First & Second Aorist Active (Punctiliar Past)

The aorist tense denotes a past action viewed as a whole without reference to duration. Features an epsilon prefix (augment) `ἐ-`.

Non-Indicative Moods: Subjunctive & Imperative

Subjunctive Mood: The mood of probability, intention, or contingency. Characterized by lengthened vowels.
Imperative Mood: The mood of direct command or supplication.

Subjunctive vs. Indicative Contrast

Advanced Syntax Metrics for High Fluency

To reach high translation accuracy, you must look past simple vocabulary paradigms and observe syntactic rules governing deep structural interpretation.

📜 Granville Sharp's Rule

When two singular, non-proper nouns are joined by a copulative conjunction (καί), and the definite article precedes the first noun but not the second, both nouns refer strictly to the same identical identity or person (e.g., "The God and Savior").

📜 Colwell's Rule

Definite predicate nouns that precede a verb usually omit the definite article. This is crucial for verifying descriptive assertions versus absolute definite descriptions in ancient philosophical claims.

📜 Prohibitive Subjunctive Syntax

When formulating a negative command ("Do not do X"), combining the negative particle μή with an Aorist Subjunctive verb issues an absolute structural ban against beginning an action, rather than just stopping an action already in progress.
